00 The Core Idea

Why most AI images look generic — and how to fix it

❌ GENERIC

"AI, create a product photo of my bag."

✅ BRANDED

"Create a product photo of my bag that embodies our minimalist aesthetic, uses warm morning light for comfort, features natural textures aligned with our sustainable values."

The difference isn't the AI tool

It's the strategic thinking, brand knowledge, and creative direction behind the prompt. AI is your creative translator — not your creative partner. You still need to know what you want.

🎯 AI is good at

Executing a clear, specific vision. Fast and cheap.

🚫 AI is bad at

Guessing your brand. "Generate something nice" = generic.

01 Brand DNA

Define your brand before you generate anything

Brand Personality & Values

Answer these before touching any AI tool:

  • What 3 words describe your brand's personality?
  • What do you stand for — and what would you never do?
  • How do you want people to feel when they see your content?
  • If your brand were a person, how would you describe them?
Minimalist Bold Playful Luxurious Raw Warm Refined Rebellious
Target Audience Deep Dive
  • Who are you reaching? (Be specific — not "everyone")
  • What are their pain points, desires, and aspirations?
  • Where do they spend time online?
  • What other brands do they love, and why?
Competitive Landscape
  • Who are your direct and indirect competitors?
  • What visual territories are oversaturated?
  • Where is the white space you can own?

Rhode succeeded because they zigged when everyone else zagged — retro sensory in a clinical skincare category.

Brand Positioning
  • What makes you different from everyone else?
  • What's your unique value proposition?
  • How do you want to be perceived in the market?

03 Prompt Builder

6 layers — build them in order. Each layer constrains the AI closer to your vision.

💡 Before writing: Read your final prompt aloud. Could an 8-year-old visualize this image? That's the level of clarity you need.
LAYER 1

The Concept

What type of image is this? Set the context before describing anything else.

Beauty shotStudio shootProduct hero shot Fashion editorialLifestyle portraitFlat lay 3D render styleCampaign visual
LAYER 2

The Subject

Describe every detail that will be visible. For people, specify all of:

ElementWhat to specify
Body & poseStance · arm position · weight distribution · energy (static/dynamic/flowing)
ClothingStyle · specific garment name · fabric type · colors · fit · condition
HairLength · texture · style · color · volume · condition
ExpressionOverall mood · eye expression · mouth · brow · cheek engagement
AccessoriesEarrings · necklaces · rings · bags · glasses
MakeupStyle (natural/dramatic/editorial) · specific details
Example: "Standing with confident posture, weight shifted to right leg, left hand on hip. Wearing a flowing navy midi dress with small white polka dots, fitted at waist with thin brown leather belt. Shoulder-length auburn hair with natural waves parted to the side."
LAYER 3

Colors & Materials

Specify the color strategy and material qualities:

Warm palette (golds, ambers, reds) Cool palette (blues, greens, purples) Neutral (whites, grays, beiges) Monochromatic

Color temperature:

Warm 2700K–3500K — cozy, golden Cool 5000K–6500K — modern, clean Neutral 3500K–5000K — balanced
LAYER 4

Composition

How the frame is structured. Don't let AI decide — specify it:

ChoiceWhen to use
Close-up / tightBeauty, skin, product detail
Medium (waist up)Model + product interaction
Full bodyFashion, lifestyle, setting
Rule of thirdsDynamic, less static
Centered / symmetricLuxury, editorial authority
Overhead / flat layProduct, food, lifestyle objects
Dutch angleEditorial tension
LAYER 5

Lighting & Mood

The single most important layer. Describe lighting with: SOURCE + DIRECTION + QUALITY + COLOR TEMP + SHADOW BEHAVIOR

Golden hour: "Warm directional sunlight from camera-left at 15° above horizon — 3000K — long soft-edged shadows stretching right — golden specular highlights on skin and hair edges"
Studio soft: "Large softbox from camera-right at 45° — 5000K — diffused even light — minimal shadows — clean catchlights in eyes"
LAYER 6

Camera & Lens

Name real cameras and lenses — they trigger visual training associations:

04 Content Types

40+ named content types — be explicit in your prompt

People-Focused (10 types)
  • Portrait — Close-up, face or upper body focus
  • Beauty Shot — Emphasis on face, skin, makeup, or hair
  • Full-Body Shot — Entire figure, fashion or lifestyle
  • Fashion Editorial — Stylized storytelling through clothing and pose
  • Street Style — Urban, candid fashion, often outdoors
  • Campaign Visual — High-end branded, marketing grade
  • Conceptual Portrait — Symbolic or artistic representation
  • Glamour Studio Shot — Elegant, dramatic styling
  • Headshot — Professional, head and shoulders
  • Lifestyle Portrait — Person in natural environment or daily activity
Studio-Based (12 types)
  • Studio Shoot — Neutral or styled backdrop, controlled lighting
  • High-Key Studio — Bright, overexposed background, soft shadows
  • Low-Key Studio — Dark background, strong highlights and contrast
  • Beauty Studio Close-up — Skin, makeup, hair in controlled lighting
  • Catalog / Lookbook — Clean, minimal product or fashion showcase
  • Commercial Product Shoot — Technical precision, brand clarity
  • Food Studio Shot — Styled food under studio conditions
  • Minimalist Studio — Sparse layout, neutral space, focus on form
  • Still Life Studio — Artistic arrangements, often luxury or decor
  • Conceptual Studio — Set design or props to evoke an idea
  • White Background Studio — Clean, e-commerce style
  • Colored Background Studio — Vibrant or branded backdrop
Product-Focused (10 types)
  • Product Still Life — Standalone with clean or styled background
  • Product in Context — Product in lifestyle or usage scene
  • Flat Lay — Top-down view of arranged items
  • Packshot — Sharp focus, packaging and brand identity
  • Luxury Product Editorial — Artistic, premium-feeling composition
  • Product Hero Shot — Dramatic, attention-grabbing presentation
  • Unboxing Visual — Product emerging from or displayed with packaging
  • Product Comparison — Multiple variations shown together
  • Ingredient / Material Focus — Close-up of components or raw materials
  • Before / After — Showing transformation or results
Environment & Mood-Based (10 types)
  • Interior Scene — Designed interior with branding elements
  • Decorative Still Life — Lifestyle scene with objects
  • Set Design Visual — Custom environments for mood storytelling
  • Brand Mood Visual — Imagery evoking brand's atmosphere or values
  • Monochrome Visual — One dominant color tone
  • Shadow Play — Artistic light and shadow interaction
  • Atmospheric Scene — Mood-driven environment, specific weather
  • Cozy Interior — Warm, inviting indoor spaces
  • Minimalist Space — Clean, sparse, focus on negative space
  • Color Story Visual — Composition built around specific palette
Artistic & Abstract (10 types)
  • Abstract Visual — Non-representational, color/shape-focused
  • Cinematic Scene — Frame that feels like a movie still
  • Surreal Composition — Dreamlike, fantastical, symbolic
  • 3D Render Style — CGI or digital render aesthetic
  • Geometric Composition — Shapes, patterns, mathematical forms
  • Texture Study — Artistic exploration of surface and material
  • Double Exposure — Layered, blended imagery
  • Vintage Film Style — Retro aesthetic, analog photography
  • Digital Art Style — Illustration blended with photography
  • Conceptual Metaphor — Visual representation of abstract ideas

05 AI Readiness Score

Where does your brand stand? Honest assessment before launching.

75–90 POINTS
🏆 AI-Ready Champion
Strong foundation, documented systems, ready to generate campaigns immediately.
55–74 POINTS
🎯 Almost There
Solid brand, some technical/strategic gaps. Note the gaps, proceed with caveats.
35–54 POINTS
🔨 Foundation First
Brand identity needs clarification before major AI campaigns. Start small, build in parallel.
0–34 POINTS
🌱 Starting Line
Beginning of the brand journey. Build the foundation correctly from day one — no bad habits to unlearn.

20 Assessment Questions

  • Do you have a clear brand identity document or style guide?
  • Can you describe your brand personality in 5 words or less?
  • Do you have a defined target audience with specific demographics?
  • Are your brand colors defined with specific HEX codes or Pantone?
  • Do you have consistent visual elements across all current content?
  • Do you create visual content regularly (at least 3× per week)?
  • Does creating a single piece take less than 2 hours?
  • Are you satisfied with the quality of your current content?
  • Do you have a content calendar or planning system?
  • Can you quickly adapt your content for different platforms?
  • Are you comfortable learning new digital tools and platforms?
  • Do you have a system for organizing and storing visual assets?
  • Do you use any design tools (Figma, Photoshop, etc.)?
  • Do you use any editing tools (CapCut, DaVinci, etc.)?
  • Do you use any AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.)?
  • Do you know what visual style your competitors use?
  • Can you identify what makes your brand visually different from competitors?
  • Do you have a content strategy aligned with business goals?
  • Are you ready to change your current content creation workflow?
  • Do you believe visual content is critical for your business growth?

07 Common Mistakes

Patterns that kill brand consistency — and how to avoid them

The Beige Trap

Choosing neutral colors and generic fonts because you're afraid to alienate anyone. Results in brands that appeal to no one and disappear into the background.

Fix: To be loved by 20%, you must be willing to be disliked by 80%. Choose visual language deliberately. Don't be afraid to alienate the wrong people to attract the right ones.

The Trend Chaser

Following every design trend makes your brand feel inconsistent and without identity. Trends come and go — your brand needs timeless elements that anchor it.

Fix: Trends can inform, not define. Reference trends selectively — only when they genuinely fit your brand vocabulary.

The Kitchen Sink Approach

Trying to communicate every benefit and appeal to every customer dilutes your message. Strong brands make choices and stick to them.

Fix: One campaign = one message. One prompt = one story.

The Copycat Strategy

Mimicking competitors' visual approach might feel safe, but guarantees you'll never stand out. Rhode succeeded because they zigged when everyone else zagged.

Fix: Analyze what's oversaturated in your category. Find the visual white space. Occupy it.

Letting AI Lead the Creative

"Generate something nice for my brand" will always produce generic content. AI does not know your brand — it knows statistical averages.

Fix: AI is your creative translator, not your creative director. You must have a clear image in mind before you write the prompt. Describe what you see, not what you want AI to figure out.

Quick Wins (implement immediately)

  • Define your color emotions — not just colors, but what each one makes people feel
  • Choose a consistent photo filter and style — apply uniform color grading rules
  • Define your lighting signature — one default lighting setup that is "your brand's light"
  • Build your anti-vocabulary — words and aesthetics you will never use
  • Write brand-context.md before your first AI image session
